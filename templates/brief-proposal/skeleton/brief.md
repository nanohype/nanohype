# Proposal Brief — __PROJECT_NAME__

## Context

You are drafting a client-facing proposal on behalf of **__COMPANY_NAME__** for **__CLIENT_NAME__**. The engagement type is **__ENGAGEMENT_TYPE__**.

Project name: **__PROJECT_NAME__**

Client context: __CLIENT_CONTEXT__

A proposal is a persuasion document disguised as a plan. Its purpose is to convince __CLIENT_NAME__ that __COMPANY_NAME__ understands their problem, has a credible solution, and will deliver measurable value. Every section must serve that purpose. The problem analysis demonstrates empathy and expertise. The solution design demonstrates capability and judgment. The pricing structure demonstrates fairness and alignment. The timeline demonstrates realism and commitment.

The engagement type determines the proposal's structure and emphasis. A services engagement emphasizes methodology, team composition, and deliverables. A product engagement emphasizes capabilities, integration, and total cost of ownership. An advisory engagement emphasizes expertise, frameworks, and outcomes. A hybrid engagement must clearly delineate which parts are services, product, and advisory, with transparent pricing for each.

If client context is provided above, it represents intelligence gathered from discovery conversations, RFP documents, or research. The proposal should demonstrate that this context was heard and understood by reflecting it in the problem analysis, solution design, and success criteria. If no client context is provided, the proposal should include a discovery section that outlines what __COMPANY_NAME__ would need to learn before finalizing the approach.

The proposal must be professional, direct, and free of filler. Every paragraph should earn its place. Avoid generic statements about "leveraging synergies" or "driving transformation." Instead, make specific claims supported by evidence, methodology, or relevant experience.

## Brief

Produce a **client-facing proposal** for __CLIENT_NAME__ from __COMPANY_NAME__.

Your analysis and drafting should cover:

- **Client needs analysis** — Analyze __CLIENT_NAME__'s situation based on available context. Identify their primary business challenge, the cost of inaction, the constraints they operate under, and what a successful outcome looks like from their perspective. Frame the problem in their language, not yours.
- **Solution design** — Propose a solution that directly addresses the identified needs. For a services engagement, define the methodology, phases, and deliverables. For a product engagement, describe capabilities, integration points, and deployment approach. Define what __COMPANY_NAME__ will deliver, what __CLIENT_NAME__ is responsible for, and where the boundaries are.
- **Approach and methodology** — Describe how the work will be executed. Define phases with clear entry and exit criteria. Specify the activities, outputs, and decision points in each phase. The methodology should feel rigorous without being rigid — show that __COMPANY_NAME__ has a proven approach while remaining responsive to what the engagement reveals.
- **Team and expertise** — Define the team structure, roles, and relevant experience. Do not list resumes — instead, describe what each role contributes to the engagement and why that expertise matters for __CLIENT_NAME__'s specific situation.
- **Pricing structure** — Design a pricing model appropriate to the engagement type. For services, define whether pricing is fixed, time-and-materials, or outcome-based, and justify the choice. For products, structure licensing, implementation, and ongoing costs. Include what is included, what is optional, and what is explicitly excluded. Pricing should align incentives — __COMPANY_NAME__ should succeed when __CLIENT_NAME__ succeeds.
- **Timeline and milestones** — Build a realistic timeline with phases, milestones, and checkpoints. Include __CLIENT_NAME__'s responsibilities and decision points — a timeline that only shows __COMPANY_NAME__'s work is incomplete. Identify the critical path and dependencies.
- **Success criteria** — Define what "done" looks like and how success will be measured. Include both project-level criteria (deliverables accepted, milestones met) and outcome-level criteria (business metrics improved, capabilities established). These criteria should be agreed upon at the start, not negotiated at the end.
- **Risk management** — Identify engagement risks and mitigation strategies. Include risks from both sides — not just technical risks, but organizational readiness, change management, resource availability, and decision-making speed.

## Output Specification

Produce a Markdown document with the following structure:

- **Cover Section** — Proposal title, __COMPANY_NAME__ and __CLIENT_NAME__ identifiers, date, engagement type, and a one-paragraph executive summary of the proposed engagement.
- **Understanding** — Restate __CLIENT_NAME__'s situation, challenges, and objectives in their terms. This section proves that __COMPANY_NAME__ listened. It should be specific enough that __CLIENT_NAME__ recognizes their own situation in it.
- **Proposed Solution** — High-level solution description, approach, and expected outcomes. Connect every element of the solution to a specific client need identified in the Understanding section.
- **Approach and Methodology** — Phase-by-phase breakdown with activities, deliverables, decision points, and responsibilities. Include a visual timeline or phase diagram description.
- **Team** — Team structure, roles, and relevant expertise. Focus on what the team brings to this specific engagement.
- **Investment** — Pricing table with line items, totals, and payment terms. Include what is included and excluded. If there are optional add-ons, present them separately.
- **Timeline** — Milestone-based timeline with dates or durations, dependencies, and __CLIENT_NAME__'s decision points.
- **Success Criteria** — Measurable criteria for project completion and business outcome evaluation.
- **Working Together** — Communication cadence, reporting structure, escalation process, and change management approach.
- **Risk and Mitigation** — Risk register with likelihood, impact, and mitigation for each identified risk.
- **Next Steps** — Concrete actions to move forward, with owners and deadlines.

## Quality Criteria

The proposal is complete and useful when:

- The Understanding section contains specific references to __CLIENT_NAME__'s situation, not generic industry observations
- The solution is traceable — every proposed activity connects to a client need, and every client need has a proposed response
- Pricing is transparent — the client can understand what they are paying for without requesting a breakdown
- The timeline includes __CLIENT_NAME__'s responsibilities and decision points, not just __COMPANY_NAME__'s deliverables
- Success criteria are measurable and agreed-upon in advance, not subjective assessments of quality
- Risks include organizational and process risks, not just technical risks
- The proposal avoids unsupported claims — every assertion of capability is backed by methodology description or relevant experience reference
- The tone is confident without being arrogant, detailed without being dense, and professional without being sterile
- A decision-maker at __CLIENT_NAME__ can evaluate the proposal without requiring a presentation — the document stands alone
- The proposal differentiates __COMPANY_NAME__ through specificity and quality of thinking, not through generic differentiator claims
