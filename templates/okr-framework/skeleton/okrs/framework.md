## What Are OKRs

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are a goal-setting framework that connects ambitious objectives to measurable outcomes. An **Objective** describes what you want to achieve — it should be qualitative, inspirational, and time-bound. **Key Results** describe how you'll know you achieved it — they should be quantitative, specific, and verifiable.

OKRs are not task lists. They measure outcomes, not output. A team can complete every task on its roadmap and still miss its OKRs if those tasks didn't produce the intended results.

---

## Principles

### 1. Outcomes Over Output

Key results measure the impact of work, not the work itself. "Ship feature X" is a task. "Reduce time-to-completion from 45 minutes to 15 minutes" is a key result.

### 2. Ambitious but Achievable

Set objectives that stretch the team beyond comfortable execution. A well-calibrated OKR should feel achievable with focused effort — not certain, not impossible. Scoring 0.7 on a well-set OKR is a strong result.

### 3. Transparent by Default

All OKRs are visible across the organization. Transparency creates alignment — teams can see how their work connects to company-level goals and identify dependencies early.

### 4. Decoupled from Compensation

OKRs are a planning and alignment tool, not a performance evaluation mechanism. Tying OKRs to bonuses incentivizes sandbagging and discourages ambition.

### 5. Fewer is Better

Each level (company, team, individual) should have 3-5 objectives with 2-4 key results each. More than that dilutes focus and makes progress tracking unmanageable.

## How to Write Good OKRs

### Writing Objectives

A good objective:
- Starts with a verb that implies direction (_Establish_, _Accelerate_, _Transform_, _Build_)
- Is qualitative and aspirational — no numbers in the objective itself
- Is achievable within one __CADENCE__ cycle
- Is understood the same way by everyone on the team

**Strong examples:**
- "Establish __ORG_NAME__ as the trusted choice for enterprise customers"
- "Accelerate developer onboarding to reduce time-to-productivity"
- "Build a self-serve analytics capability that eliminates ad-hoc data requests"

**Weak examples:**
- "Increase revenue" — too vague, no direction
- "Ship the new dashboard" — this is a task, not an outcome
- "Be the best product in the market" — unmeasurable, not time-bound

### Writing Key Results

A good key result:
- Is quantitative with a specific number or threshold
- Has a clear starting point (baseline) and target
- Is measurable within the __CADENCE__ cycle
- Can be verified by anyone with access to the data source
- Measures the outcome, not the activity

**Strong examples:**
- "Increase enterprise trial-to-paid conversion from 12% to 20%"
- "Reduce median onboarding time from 14 days to 5 days"
- "Achieve Net Promoter Score of 50+ from current baseline of 35"

**Weak examples:**
- "Launch 3 new features" — activity metric, not outcome
- "Improve customer satisfaction" — no number, not verifiable
- "Reduce bugs by 50%" — measures output quality, not user outcome

## OKR Levels

### Company-Level OKRs

Set by the executive team. Define the 3-5 most important outcomes for __ORG_NAME__ this __CADENCE__ cycle. These are the strategic priorities that every team should be able to trace their work back to.

**Ownership:** CEO / executive team
**Review cadence:** __CADENCE__ formal review, monthly progress check

### Team-Level OKRs

Set by team leads in collaboration with their teams. Each team OKR should connect to at least one company-level objective. Teams own 3-5 objectives that represent their contribution to the company strategy.

**Ownership:** Team lead
**Review cadence:** __CADENCE__ formal review, bi-weekly check-in

### Individual-Level OKRs

Optional — most effective for senior individual contributors and managers. Individual OKRs should connect to team-level objectives and focus on personal growth or specific high-impact contributions.

**Ownership:** Individual + manager
**Review cadence:** __CADENCE__ formal review, weekly 1:1 check-in

## Alignment Map

```
Company Objective 1
├── Team A Objective 1 (directly supports)
│   ├── Individual OKR (contributor)
│   └── Individual OKR (contributor)
├── Team B Objective 2 (directly supports)
└── Team C Objective 1 (partially supports)

Company Objective 2
├── Team A Objective 2 (directly supports)
└── Team D Objective 1 (directly supports)
```

Every team OKR should trace to a company objective. If a team OKR doesn't connect, either the company objectives are incomplete or the team work isn't strategically aligned — both are worth surfacing.

## Lifecycle

### 1. Drafting (Week 1 of cycle)

- Executive team publishes company-level OKRs
- Team leads draft team OKRs in response
- Individuals draft personal OKRs (if applicable)

### 2. Alignment Review (Week 2 of cycle)

- Cross-team review to identify dependencies and conflicts
- Adjust key results to avoid double-counting or gaps
- Final OKRs published and visible to all

### 3. Execution (Weeks 3 through end of cycle)

- Teams track progress against key results
- Regular check-ins surface blockers and adjust tactics (not objectives)
- Mid-cycle review at halfway point for calibration

### 4. Scoring and Retrospective (Final week of cycle)

- Score each key result on 0-1.0 scale
- Objective score = average of its key result scores
- Retrospective: what drove the scores, what would we do differently

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It Happens | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Too many OKRs | Teams try to capture everything they do | Limit to 3-5 objectives, 2-4 key results each |
| Sandbagging | Targets set conservatively to guarantee success | Normalize 0.7 as a strong score; celebrate ambition |
| Roofshot only | All OKRs are safe, incremental improvements | Include at least one "moonshot" objective per cycle |
| Activity-based KRs | Key results describe tasks instead of outcomes | Ask "if we do this but it doesn't work, do we still score?" |
| Set and forget | OKRs drafted then ignored until scoring | Regular check-ins keep OKRs as a living tool |
| No baseline | Targets set without knowing the starting point | Require baseline measurement for every key result |
