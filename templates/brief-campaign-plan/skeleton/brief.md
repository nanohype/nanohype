# Campaign Plan Brief — __PROJECT_NAME__

## Context

You are producing a marketing campaign plan for **__PROJECT_NAME__**.

Campaign goal: **__CAMPAIGN_GOAL__**

Target audience: **__TARGET_AUDIENCE__**

Budget: __BUDGET__

Timeline: __TIMELINE__

A campaign plan is the operational document that turns a marketing objective into a coordinated execution across channels, messages, and milestones. It is not a brand strategy, a creative brief, or a content calendar — though it informs all of those. The plan defines *what* the campaign will accomplish, *who* it will reach, *where* and *when* it will execute, and *how* success is measured.

The campaign goal above drives every decision in the plan. An awareness campaign optimizes for reach and recall. An acquisition campaign optimizes for conversion and cost-per-acquisition. A retention campaign optimizes for engagement depth and churn reduction. A launch campaign coordinates across all of these with time-bound intensity. Your plan should align every channel, message, and budget allocation to the stated goal.

The target audience is not a demographic. It is a group of people with specific needs, behaviors, information consumption patterns, and decision-making processes. Your audience analysis should go deep enough to inform channel selection and message framing — not just "marketing managers" but "marketing managers at B2B SaaS companies with 50-200 employees who are evaluating their first attribution tool."

If budget is specified, allocate it across channels with clear rationale. If budget is not specified, structure the plan in tiers (minimum viable, recommended, and aggressive) so the stakeholder can choose the investment level. If timeline is specified, build the execution calendar around it. If not, recommend a timeline based on the goal and channel mix.

## Brief

Produce a **campaign plan** for __PROJECT_NAME__.

Your strategy development and planning should cover:

- **Goal refinement** — Sharpen the campaign goal into specific, measurable objectives. Translate __CAMPAIGN_GOAL__ into concrete targets (e.g., "generate 500 marketing-qualified leads in Q3" rather than "increase leads"). Define the primary conversion action and secondary engagement actions.
- **Audience analysis** — Decompose __TARGET_AUDIENCE__ into segments with distinct needs and channel preferences. For each segment, define: demographic and firmographic attributes, psychographic drivers, content consumption habits, decision-making journey, and key objections or barriers.
- **Channel strategy** — Select distribution channels based on audience behavior, not availability. For each channel, specify: role in the funnel (awareness, consideration, conversion), expected reach, content format requirements, frequency and cadence, and budget allocation. Justify channel selection against alternatives.
- **Messaging framework** — Define the core narrative, key messaging pillars, and proof points. Create message variants for each audience segment and funnel stage. The messaging should address the audience's problem first, position the solution second, and differentiate from alternatives third.
- **Content requirements** — Specify the content assets needed to execute the campaign. For each asset, define: format, channel, audience segment, funnel stage, key message, call-to-action, and production requirements. Identify content that can be repurposed across channels.
- **Budget allocation** — Distribute budget across channels, content production, tools, and contingency. For each line item, provide the allocation amount, expected return, and the assumption behind the projection. Include a contingency reserve for mid-campaign optimization.
- **KPI framework** — Define metrics for each funnel stage and channel. Distinguish between vanity metrics (impressions, followers) and actionable metrics (cost-per-acquisition, conversion rate, pipeline influenced). Set targets that connect to the campaign goal.
- **Execution timeline** — Build a week-by-week or phase-by-phase calendar with milestones, content drops, channel activations, and measurement checkpoints. Include ramp-up, peak, and wind-down phases.

## Output Specification

Produce a Markdown document with the following structure:

- **Executive Summary** — Campaign name, goal, audience, timeline, budget, and three-sentence strategy summary. A stakeholder should be able to approve or redirect from this section alone.
- **Campaign Objectives** — SMART objectives derived from the campaign goal, with primary and secondary conversion actions.
- **Audience Profiles** — Segment-level profiles with needs, behaviors, channel preferences, and objection maps.
- **Channel Plan** — Table of channels with role, format, cadence, budget allocation, and expected performance. Include a channel-mix rationale.
- **Messaging Framework** — Core narrative, pillars, proof points, and segment-specific message variants. Include sample copy for the highest-priority channel and segment.
- **Content Matrix** — Table of content assets with format, channel, segment, funnel stage, message, CTA, and production status.
- **Budget Breakdown** — Line-item budget with allocations, expected returns, and assumptions. Include total and per-channel cost-per-result projections.
- **KPI Dashboard** — Metric definitions, measurement methods, targets, and reporting cadence. Organized by funnel stage.
- **Execution Calendar** — Phase-by-phase timeline with milestones, deliverables, and owners.
- **Optimization Plan** — Decision triggers for mid-campaign adjustments. Define what signals indicate a channel is underperforming and what the reallocation protocol is.
- **Risk and Contingency** — Campaign-specific risks (audience fatigue, channel policy changes, competitive launches) with contingency responses.

## Quality Criteria

The campaign plan is complete and useful when:

- Campaign objectives are SMART (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound) and traceable back to the stated goal
- Audience segments are specific enough to inform copy and creative decisions — not just demographic labels
- Channel selection is justified by audience behavior data or reasoned hypotheses, not by convention or completeness
- Budget allocations include the rationale and assumptions behind each projection, not just dollar amounts
- The KPI framework distinguishes between metrics that inform optimization decisions and metrics that report outcomes
- The execution calendar is specific enough that a marketing coordinator could begin production from it without further briefing
- Content requirements specify the purpose and CTA of each asset, not just the format
- The optimization plan defines concrete decision triggers (e.g., "if CPA exceeds $X by week 3, reallocate Y% from channel A to channel B"), not just "monitor and adjust"
- Risk mitigation addresses campaign-specific scenarios, not generic marketing risks
- The plan is internally consistent — budget, timeline, content volume, and channel cadence are all compatible with each other
